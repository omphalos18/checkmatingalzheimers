Our Mission

A Project Rooted
in Love & Family

When Caton Tsao watched both of his grandparents lose their memories to Alzheimer's disease, he decided to act. Together with fellow expert chess player Allen Hong, he launched Checkmating Alzheimer's — a student-led initiative in Upstate South Carolina that turns a passion for chess into a powerful force against dementia.

The project operates on three pillars: fundraising through community chess tournaments, volunteering by teaching chess at retirement communities, and research through a partnership with Clemson University studying chess as a cognitive protective factor.

"Personally, I have witnessed the profound impact of the disease on the patient and the family. With this project, I hoped to help others in my community since I couldn't help my own family member on another continent."
— Caton Tsao, Founder

Science & Research

Chess & the Brain:
The Research

Partnering with Clemson University to study whether chess can protect against cognitive decline.

🧠

Hippocampus

Chess engages the hippocampus for memory formation and recall — the same region most affected by Alzheimer's.

🎯

Prefrontal Cortex

Planning moves ahead activates the prefrontal cortex, strengthening executive function and strategic thinking.

👁

Occipital Lobe

Visualizing board positions before moving engages the occipital lobe, training spatial reasoning and pattern recognition.

CU

Clemson University Cognitive Study

Caton Tsao and Aman Sreshta proposed a cross-sectional research study to Dr. Kaileigh Byrne, Associate Professor of Cognitive Psychology at Clemson University. The study compares cognitive function in adults aged 65+ who have played chess for years versus those who have not — aiming to produce publishable, peer-reviewed findings.
Recruiting: Ages 65+ Contact: decisionlab@clemson.edu Cross-sectional design
📊 Research Finding

A 2019 NIH study found that chess practice can be considered a protective factor against dementia and cognitive decline in older adults, particularly through the enhancement of cognitive reserve. Yet Dr. Byrne notes: "Surprisingly, there is almost no work specifically on chess, and it is surprising because it engages so many different cognitive functions."
